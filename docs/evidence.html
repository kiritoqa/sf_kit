AI governance96/100

AI / RAG quality governance

Problem

AI products can't be validated only with fixed assertions. Answers may be semantically correct with different wording, or confidently wrong while looking plausible.

What I built

QA approaches using golden datasets, measurable thresholds, semantic review, expert UAT, latency checks, hallucination risk controls, human ownership, and documented evidence for each release decision.

Why it matters

Teams get a way to decide whether an AI feature is objectively testable, requires expert judgment, or needs evidence-backed release governance. "QA-complete" and "release-ready" become two separate, clearly owned decisions.

Requirements94/100

QA Spec Kit: story-to-evidence workflow

Problem

Requirements, test cases, bugs, and decisions often live in disconnected places. QA loses context, automation starts from assumptions, and later discussions repeat the same uncertainty.

What I built

A story workflow where Jira context moves into QA analysis, BA questions, updated specifications, test cases, review, user-flow maps, dependency graphs, and automation-ready outputs. Session state preserves context across the cycle.

Why it matters

QA documentation becomes a working delivery artifact. The path from story to "QA-complete" and "release-ready" is documented, connected, and repeatable.

AI governance95/100

AI-assisted QA quality standard

Problem

Teams can use AI quickly and still produce weak outputs: invented business rules, missing edge cases, vague bugs, unstable tests, or unvalidated assumptions.

What I built

AI-assisted QA guardrails: input quality rules (S1–S5 gates), design readiness, prompt structure, output validation, human review, audit trail, privacy rules, escalation, and a team training program with four workshops, practical assessment, and retrospective.

Why it matters

AI becomes a controlled QA accelerator. The team gains speed while keeping professional ownership of every result — not an experiment but a disciplined workflow.

Automation93/100

26,000 generated test cases from dynamic data

Problem

Some projects require high-volume coverage across dynamic data combinations. Writing and maintaining every case manually creates cost, delay, and inconsistency.

What I built

Automation and data-driven approaches that generated large volumes of test cases from dynamic inputs. QA effort shifts from manual creation to pattern design, data validation, and review.

Why it matters

Repeated testing patterns become scalable systems. Coverage grows with the product without growing in proportion to the number of features.

Automation91/100

1,000+ tests in 120 hours: pattern-based automation

Problem

Products with many similar forms lead to duplicated test logic and expensive maintenance if each flow is automated separately.

What I built

Reusable steps, randomized data patterns, and pattern-based automation for repeated form structures. 116 similar forms reduced to maintainable patterns, producing 1,000+ automated checks in 120 hours.

Why it matters

Automation becomes maintainable at scale. The team expands coverage without multiplying code and maintenance cost.

Visual quality89/100

Visual regression across environments

Problem

UI regressions may not break APIs or functional assertions, but users still see layout issues, missing sections, responsive problems, or unexpected differences across environments.

What I built

Visual comparison approaches that open pages across environments, capture screenshots, compare differences, handle viewports/authentication/cookies, and produce reports for review. Jenkins integration for scheduled or pre-release execution.

Why it matters

Visual quality becomes repeatable and reviewable before release. Teams see where a page changed, on which viewport, and in which environment.

Accessibility88/100

Accessibility reporting and Section 508 triage

Problem

Accessibility reports can be large, repetitive, and difficult for teams to convert into actionable work. CSV and Word exports create more confusion than clarity.

What I built

Reporting pipelines processing SortSite exports and Axe+Playwright scans: deduplication, classification, grouping by violation type and page element, HTML summaries, and team-ready outputs for WCAG/Section 508 contexts.

Why it matters

Accessibility becomes manageable work. Teams prioritize repeated issues, track progress, and discuss fixes concretely instead of navigating an unreadable report.

Defects90/100

Structured defect intelligence

Problem

Weak bug reports create expensive back-and-forth. Developers need environment, steps, expected/actual behavior, screenshots, severity, and clear reproduction context — not a one-line description.

What I built

Structured defect workflows turning raw QA observations into reproducible Jira issues with required fields, story linkage, screenshots, and validation rules. If required information is missing, the workflow asks what to capture — not producing a weak defect.

Why it matters

Defects become better communication between QA, developers, PMs, and stakeholders. Reproduction is faster and defect quality becomes consistent across the whole team.

Automation91/100

Test batch lifecycle and QA automation review

Problem

Automation becomes chaotic when tests, branches, Jira statuses, PRs, and reviews are disconnected. Each engineer works independently, and the automation base becomes fragile.

What I built

A lifecycle model for test batches — story-level branches, PR review standards with code quality expectations, merge workflow, Jira handoff, and automation quality criteria. Story-level branching instead of per-batch to reduce code chaos.

Why it matters

QA automation becomes an engineering practice with maintainability, review quality, and traceability — not a collection of isolated scripts that each engineer owns individually.

Leadership92/100

Weekly QA health reporting

Problem

Jira status alone can hide reality. Work may be visible in branches, commits, PRs, QA time, defects, or blockers before it is reflected in task status. Leadership asks "how are we doing?" and gets an optimistic summary.

What I built

A weekly QA report framework connecting Jira, repository activity, QA time tracking, defects, timelines, trends, and AI-assisted assessment of requirements-to-code coverage. Standalone HTML document with 9 sections and trend history.

Why it matters

Leadership gets a more truthful view: what is actually moving, where status diverges from reality, where deadlines overlap, and where QA capacity is going.

Requirements90/100

Multi-domain test strategy

Problem

One test strategy template does not fit every product. Enterprise data, healthcare migrations, compliance tools, and AI products have fundamentally different risks.

What I built

Risk-based strategies for enterprise data platforms (BI, DWH, roles, access), healthcare/CMS migrations (production-as-oracle, UAT, go-live discipline), social/compliance tools (calculations, niche users, load assumptions), and AI/RAG products (semantic quality, golden datasets, hallucination control).

Why it matters

Quality work aligns with each product's real risk profile instead of copying a familiar template. The right risks get covered; the wrong ones don't waste capacity.

Leadership88/100

QA team enablement and practice development

Problem

Quality cannot scale through one strong individual. Teams need standards, examples, expectations, coaching, and a shared language to grow consistently.

What I built

QA standards, role expectations, live sessions, onboarding templates, mentoring approach, interview participation (20+), manual-to-automation development path, AI workflow enablement, and practical workshops with assessment and retrospective.

Why it matters

Quality becomes a team capability. People understand the quality bar, how to communicate risks, how to structure defects, when to escalate, and how to grow independently.

Complete evidence map

62 signal blocks — everything I built and introduced

Grouped by focus area. Each block includes the context and magnetism score. Expand any group to read the full signals.

1. Early requirements analysis system 90/100

QA connects before active testing begins: checks acceptance criteria, design, roles, data, integrations, empty states, error states, access rights, and dependencies. If test cases can't be written without guessing, the story isn't ready. Reduces late clarifications, rework, and disputed defects.

2. Turning requirements into a QA specification 92/100

Collects Jira context, finds unclear language, forms questions for BA/PM, and creates a structured QA specification with conditions, data, roles, expected behavior, negative scenarios, coverage boundaries, assumptions, and gaps. Becomes a working bridge between requirements, testing, automation, and maintenance.

3. Reverse loop: specification back to Jira 87/100

From the finished specification: creates tasks and checklists in Jira, preserves the link between document and backlog, updates state, and maintains traceability. Solves the most expensive delivery problem — sync loss between what was decided, built, and verified.

4. Large-scale test generation from repeating patterns 91/100

Repeating steps converted to reusable functions and data templates. 1,000+ automated checks in 120 hours; 116 similar forms reduced to maintainable patterns. Coverage scales without multiplying maintenance cost.

5. Large-volume test case generation and data validation 93/100

26,000 generated test cases in one documented project context. QA effort shifts from manual copying to pattern design, validation, and review. The approach is the signal — not the raw number.

36. NZDPU: automated test generation and data validation system 93/100

Large-scale data checking built as a system rather than executed as repetitive manual work. Shows the ability to build data verification infrastructure.

37. RHL: speed of automation under budget constraint 88/100

Approximately 1,700 automated tests in under one month. 190 of 350 allocated hours used. Strong signal for speed, budget discipline, and the ability to build coverage quickly without compromise on structure.

38. Create & Assess: reducing manual QA ratio to development time 91/100

Manual QA effort at approximately 5–8% of development time versus a typical 20–30%. Shows predictability and manageable cost of quality when automation and process work together.

6. Automatic page comparison across environments 89/100

Framework opens pages in different environments, makes screenshots, compares visual differences, and produces a report. Configuration via files — no code changes needed for new sites. Runs locally or via Jenkins.

7. Multi-page visual check with different screen sizes 86/100

URL lists, different screen sizes, authorization, cookies, login flows, and CI execution. Problems visible only on mobile, tablet, or specific environment are caught before release.

8. Accessibility reporting and standard compliance 88/100

Processes SortSite exports: cleans data, removes duplicates, extracts accessibility sections, matches similar descriptions, classifies by type, and prepares team-ready files. Turns a heavy report into an actionable list.

9. Automated browser accessibility scan 82/100

System crawls page lists, collects accessibility violations, groups by type and element, shows statistics, and produces HTML reports. Honest framing: auto-scan doesn't replace a full audit but provides a strong initial triage picture.

10. Unified defect formatting standard 90/100

Rules and tools turning raw QA notes into structured defects: project, environment, story link, screenshots, expected result, actual result, steps, severity, additional conditions. If required info is missing, the system returns questions instead of creating a weak bug.

11. Defects as structured data objects 88/100

Bugs as structured objects with fields, templates, validation, attachments, and story links. Enables Jira API creation and consistent defect quality at scale. Less chaos in bug tracking; faster reproducibility.

12. Browser automation based on real page structure 91/100

First: plan page states. Then: structured snapshot of the interface. Then: locator layer built from the snapshot. Specification says what to check; snapshot shows how elements are actually represented; project rules define stable code. Reduces test fragility significantly.

13. Separating interface capture from test execution 88/100

Interface study in IDE separated from mass test execution in terminal or CI. Tests don't depend on manual presence of a specific engineer. Makes automation suitable for real team use.

14. QA processes with AI under human control 95/100

AI assists with requirements, questions, test scenarios, defects, reports — but the final decision stays with the human. Input rules, output checks, quality gates, and degradation signals all described. Companies want AI but fear loss of control. This directly addresses that.

15. Organizational AI-QA standard with S1–S5 gates 95/100

Normative standard: requirements quality, design readiness, prompt structure, mandatory result validation, human oversight, audit trail, team competencies. Not "using AI" — a managed quality model for AI-assisted work. One of the strongest blocks for Head of QA positioning.

16. Prompt Library as a team standard 93/100

Library of templates for typical QA tasks: story analysis, questions, test cases, bugs, checklists, strategy. Value is not in the prompts themselves — the team gets a repeatable way to give AI the right context and receive artifacts of consistent quality.

17. Team AI training through workshops and assessment 92/100

Training program: kick-off, live demos, 4 workshops, independent practice, practical assessment, retrospective, documentation update. Shows mature change management: the tool is introduced through learning, practice, and skill verification — not a mandate.

27. Strategic AI-QA vision for leadership 92/100

Strategic vision for AI-QA on a 2026–2027 horizon: why implement AI, what risks, what phases, what KPIs, what sponsorship is needed. Shows the ability to speak with leadership at the strategy, roadmap, and operational decisions level.

28. QA operating model: project → requirements → story → release 89/100

Quality managed at multiple levels: project strategy, requirements preparation, story-level checks, gates, reports, regression, release readiness. The central block for Head of QA.

29. Two-track transformation: system and people 87/100

Separates implementation into two tracks: build the system (workflow, standards, integrations, template library) and teach people to use it (workshops, practice, assessment, retro). Strong change management model.

30. Contractual QA for AI/RAG portal 96/100

Quality verified not by opinion but by thresholds: search accuracy, response latency, absence of critical hallucinations, golden dataset, expert UAT validation. Rare and very strong case — shows QA for non-deterministic systems. Highest scored block.

31. Four quality layers for AI systems 91/100

API checks, dataset quality of answers, browser scenarios, visual verification, and expert UAT. Avoids reducing AI verification to "opened a chat and asked a few questions." Valuable for companies starting to build AI products.

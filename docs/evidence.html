Curated evidence

Eight case studies that show how quality systems are built.

This page is intentionally selective. The goal is to make the strongest proof easy to understand: what problem existed, what system was built, and why that capability matters on a project.

Competitor-informed structure

Less archive, more signal

Strong QA/SDET portfolios usually work best with a small number of deep, clear cases. These examples were selected because they show practical judgment, system thinking, and evidence of delivery value.

AI product QA

AI quality is a contract, not a demo

Problem

A maritime / regulated-adjacent portal used semantic search and a RAG chatbot. Quality could not be reduced to “a few questions looked fine”: LLM behavior is non-deterministic, latency matters, multilingual answers matter, and hallucinations can look confident.

What was built / shaped

A QA model with API-first validation, golden dataset thinking, measurable thresholds, groundedness / faithfulness checks, p50/p95 latency thinking, expert UAT, escalation paths, and documented release evidence. The public-safe wording is anonymized; client names and internal milestones are not exposed.

Why it matters to a team

Most teams can demo AI. Fewer teams can decide whether an AI release is safe, measurable, and owned. This case shows AI uncertainty turned into a quality governance model that leadership can actually use.

AI governance

AI-assisted QA without losing human ownership

Problem

AI can speed up QA and still produce weak work: invented business rules, missing edge cases, vague bugs, unstable tests, or outputs that no one feels responsible for.

What was built / shaped

A quality standard for AI-assisted QA: requirements readiness, design readiness, prompt structure, S1–S5 validation gates, human review, audit trail, privacy awareness, degradation signals, and team enablement through workshops and practical assessment.

Why it matters to a team

Companies want AI speed but fear AI chaos. This system gives both speed and control: faster artifacts, clearer review gates, and explicit human accountability for the result.

Requirements

From story to evidence: SpecForge

Problem

Requirements, test cases, bugs, decisions, and coverage often live in different places. QA starts from assumptions, automation starts from unstable context, and the team pays for the same uncertainty repeatedly.

What was built / shaped

SpecForge: a story-to-evidence workflow in Cursor. Jira context moves into analysis, BA questions, QA specification, test cases, review, user-flow map, dependency graph, coverage checks, and automation-ready output. The specification becomes a living delivery artifact, not a static document.

Why it matters to a team

This is the kind of system that changes QA from “test after development” into “risk discovery before commitment.” It saves time because it prevents confusion from entering the sprint.

Automation architecture

Spec says what. Snapshots say how.

Problem

End-to-end automation often becomes brittle because tests are written from assumptions: guessed selectors, copied text locators, hidden auth flows, and manual fixes inside test files after every UI change.

What was built / shaped

A spec-linked, snapshot-driven Playwright model: the specification defines what user behavior matters; MCP captures real page structure; Page Objects are generated from actual snapshots; CI runs tests independently from the capture layer. Locator fixes belong in Page Objects, not random test patches.

Why it matters to a team

This is modern automation architecture, not “record and hope.” It reduces locator debt, makes tests more explainable, and keeps the human responsible for specification and release decisions.

Visual quality

VisualGuard: visual regression as release confidence

Problem

UI regressions do not always break functional tests. Layout shifts, missing sections, wrong responsive behavior, auth-specific pages, and environment differences can reach users while dashboards stay green.

What was built / shaped

A configurable visual regression framework using Java, Playwright, JSON site configs, multiple viewports, auth handlers, screenshot-only mode, Allure reports, parallel execution, and Jenkins execution. It can compare dev/staging/prod or produce visual review artifacts.

Why it matters to a team

It turns visual quality from subjective last-minute checking into repeatable evidence. Teams can see what changed, where, and under which environment conditions.

Accessibility

Accessibility debt as an operational pipeline

Problem

Accessibility reports can be huge, repetitive, and hard to convert into engineering work. Raw exports often create more confusion than decisions.

What was built / shaped

SortSite processing and Axe + Playwright reporting: CSV/DOCX normalization, fuzzy matching, duplicate reduction, WCAG A/AA categorization, grouping by issue type and element, HTML summaries, and Jenkins-ready processing. In one documented context, processing went from roughly 3 hours to 15–45 minutes per site.

Why it matters to a team

Compliance work becomes manageable and prioritized. Instead of handing developers a wall of issues, QA gives them a structured backlog and a way to track progress.

Scale

26,000 test cases without 26,000 manual decisions

Problem

Some products need high-volume coverage across dynamic data or repeating structures. Manual creation becomes slow, inconsistent, and expensive.

What was built / shaped

Data-driven and pattern-based automation: 26,000 generated test cases in one project context; 1,000+ automated tests in 120 hours by reducing 116 similar forms into reusable patterns; approximately 1,700 tests automated in under one month in another context.

Why it matters to a team

The impressive part is not the number alone. It is the ability to recognize repetition, turn it into a system, and scale coverage without scaling manual effort at the same rate.

Leadership visibility

What Jira says vs. what is really happening

Problem

Jira status can look healthy while branches, commits, PRs, QA time, defects, and blockers tell another story. Leadership needs project health, not only ticket status.

What was built / shaped

A weekly QA health reporting framework concept: standalone HTML report, Jira + Bitbucket + Harvest + defects + timelines, deadline overlap detection, trend history, and AI-assisted review of requirement-to-code coverage.

Why it matters to a team

This is QA leadership as decision support. It helps leadership see risk early, understand capacity, and act before “green status” becomes a late release surprise.

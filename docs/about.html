Professional biography

The full story

My name is Serghei Fortuna. I'm a Senior QA Automation Engineer and QA Lead. My work sits at the intersection of practical testing, automation, process design, team enablement, and building tools that make product quality more manageable and visible.

I came into QA through manual testing — and that foundation still matters. Manual testing teaches you to see the product through a user's eyes, notice non-obvious scenarios, challenge requirements, and understand where system behavior can break a real user path. When I moved into automation, I kept that connection to the product and to real risk. Automation, for me, has always been a way to preserve the team's knowledge, remove repetitive work, and make quality verification more stable — not a replacement for thinking.

Over time my focus widened. I started looking at QA as a system: how a team receives requirements, how it understands story readiness, how it records open questions to BA and PM, how it chooses coverage depth, how it writes defects, how it understands the release state, how it tracks what's really moving in Jira and the repository, how it onboards new QA engineers, and how it uses AI without losing professional accountability for the result.

Three core lines

What I'm strongest at

One of the strongest parts of my experience is building QA processes for AI systems. I worked with approaches where the quality of an AI/RAG product is not validated by a subjective impression from a few questions, but through thresholds, golden datasets, expert UAT validation, hallucination risk control, latency checks, documented evidence, and clear release decision ownership.

In these systems it's important to separate what a script can verify — API behavior, UI flows, latency, dataset coverage — from what requires expert semantic assessment: answer quality, factual accuracy, contract compliance. That separation is what makes "QA-complete" and "release-ready" two different, clearly owned decisions, not one ambiguous status.

I also built the internal AI-assisted QA layer for regular delivery work: input rules, prompt libraries, output validation, audit trail, and a training program covering kick-off, live demos, four workshops, practical assessment, and retrospective. The goal was for every QA engineer to understand where AI can be trusted, where results need human review, and when to escalate.

On most projects, knowledge lives in fragments: part in Jira, part in comments, part in design, part in the BA's head, part in the QA engineer's notes. I built approaches where these fragments are collected into a structured QA specification.

That specification records conditions, roles, data, expected behavior, negative scenarios, coverage boundaries, assumptions, open questions, dependencies, and risks. It becomes not a decorative document but a working bridge between requirements, testing, automation, and future maintenance — and it can generate Jira tasks, checklists, and test batches while preserving traceability.

The reverse direction matters too: from specification back into Jira, maintaining the connection between what was decided, what was built, and what was verified. This solves one of the most expensive problems in delivery: the sync loss between three separate spaces.

I don't treat high-volume testing as a reason to write hundreds of similar individual checks. When I see repetition — similar forms, recurring flows, dynamic data sets, predictable patterns — I look for the underlying structure and build a system that covers the volume.

In one documented context: pattern-based automation produced more than 1,000 automated tests in 120 hours for 116 similar forms. In another: 26,000 test cases generated from dynamic data inputs, shifting the QA effort from manual creation to pattern design, validation, and review.

The approach means QA can grow with the product without growing in proportion to the number of features.

Where my experience formed

An agency with parallel projects and different domains

My professional experience formed in an agency environment where several projects, domains, teams, clients, constraints, and requirement maturity levels can run at the same time. In different periods I led QA on several parallel projects — in peak moments up to ten streams.

That environment makes personal attention alone unsustainable. You need processes, rules, templates, tools, shared terminology, and the ability to quickly identify where the main risk of each specific project is. Everything I've built comes from that pressure.

Working style

Calm, systematic, context-first

I start by understanding the product, the users, the risks, and the constraints. I ask: what are we building, for whom, where is the risk, what are the limitations, who makes the decision, what's already known, what needs clarifying, what can be automated, and what requires manual judgment?

Sometimes the problem looks like "not enough testers" but the root cause is in requirements. Sometimes it seems like automation is needed but first you need to stabilize the data and agree on readiness criteria. Sometimes the team asks for a report but what it really needs is a clear model for making the release decision.

How I communicate with different stakeholders

One goal, many languages

With QA: coverage, scenarios, data, defects, risk, reproducibility.

With developers: logs, environments, test stability, locators, PR review, failure causes.

With PM/BA: requirements, priorities, scope, acceptance criteria, blockers.

With leadership: release readiness, cost of chaos, capacity, risks, and why process matters.

What can I do today so that tomorrow the team works a little better?

This question explains almost every initiative I've started: early requirements analysis, QA specifications, test generation, visual regression checks, accessibility reports, defect standards, browser automation, AI quality gates, team training, weekly health reporting, QA as a Service, and multi-domain test strategies.

The boundary between process and bureaucracy matters. A process is useful when it reduces uncertainty, helps people make decisions, preserves knowledge, and makes quality repeatable. A process is harmful when it exists for form and increases load without a clear outcome. My approach is always pragmatic: find where the team is losing time, and build the minimum sufficient system that makes tomorrow better than today.
